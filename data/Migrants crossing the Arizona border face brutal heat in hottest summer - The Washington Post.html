A migrant was left behind in the desert. Volunteers braved scorching heat to try to find him.

Heat has long threatened migrants crossing the Arizona desert. Now, amid the hottest years on record, it is putting missions to recover their bodies in jeopardy.

AJO, Ariz. The young Mexican soldier hefted his camouflage backpack and stepped through a hole in the border wall.

The temperature was 99 degrees and rising. Jos Antonio Salinas Pineda was carrying more than 40 pounds, much of it water. The Sonoran Desert lay ahead. He felt confident, he told his sister. On his military missions to burn poppy fields and hunt drug labs, Salinas had often camped out in rugged mountains, once surviving for a week with little food. He was now 21 his birth date tattooed on his chest in Roman numerals and strong.

His group five migrants, led by a teenage smuggler was scheduled to arrive in Phoenix, more than 100 miles away, by 5 p.m. the following day.

Today we begin to walk, he texted his family minutes before setting off in early June.

A day and a half later, slumped beneath a cactus, swollen and blistered, he could go no farther. The first rays of sun were rearing up over the horizon. The others shouldered their packs and walked away.

During the worlds hottest summer on record, in one of the hottest parts of the country, a group went looking for Salinas. On three separate attempts in June, August and September volunteers from the Armadillos Search and Rescue SD humanitarian group hiked more than 40 miles across the desert and up mountains.

While heat here has long threatened migrants, escalating temperatures are now putting even recovery missions in jeopardy. As the Armadillos hunted for some sign of Salinas, they entered a furnace that would leave some of them vomiting and gasping, with throbbing heads and racing hearts.

Im an avid hiker, said Ernesto Macias, a 27-year-old emergency room nurse who recognized his own early symptoms of heat stroke on one trip. This is a whole different beast.

So far this year, there have been 61 days above 110 degrees in the Phoenix area, more than in any year on record. The city has hit 100 degrees for 113 days in a row this year, more than a month longer than any comparable stretch.

The remains of more than 3,000 people have been found since 2000 just in the swath of Pima County desert that Salinas was traversing. The leading cause of death is exposure to the elements, primarily heat, according to migrant death records collected by the nonprofit Humane Borders in partnership with the Pima County Office of the Medical Examiner.

Pima County was enduring the hottest summer on record. The average reading of 87.3 degrees was more than four degrees hotter than the 20th century mean temperature.

We have to do it now because this is the time when people really get sick, get injured, said Roque Mateo, a 62-year-old who runs a plant nursery north of San Diego and participated in those searches for Salinas. His godson had disappeared a decade ago, one of 43 students pulled off buses in Guerrero, a defining atrocity in Mexicos recent history.

Salinas left Mexico motivated by fear of the same drug cartels he pursued as a soldier. Local gangs in his home state of Morelos had threatened him because of his military work, his family said. He also harbored dreams of a better life. He hoped to work as a landscaper in Los Angeles and save money for his return. On a leave from his unit, he traveled to the border.

Were here praying for you, Lucero Salinas wrote to her brother on June 1, the day he crossed. You are strong and young. You will be able to do this. Dont ever give up.

Im going now, were the last words to his family. I love you all.

By the time his sister replied, I love you brother!!! Salinas had turned off his phone and started to run.

It is very, very hot

The Armadillos formed a circle in a parking lot of the Organ Pipe Cactus National Monument at dawn, 68 days after Salinas had been abandoned. It was their second search for him.

There were 11 of them, including a landscaper, a cabinet maker and an engineer. They wore long-sleeved blue shirts and straw hats. Some strapped on shin guards for the snakes. These volunteers had driven several hours in beat-up vans from San Diego after work on a Friday, slept briefly or not at all outside a barn, and woke in darkness to cover ground before the heat mounted.

They are one of several humanitarian groups arrayed along the border who look for those lost in the desert. Many of them crossed the same desert to get to the United States years ago and know its dangers.

Roberto Resendiz, a 47-year-old construction worker, addressed the group in Spanish before they set out. He stood with hands clasped in front of him and bowed his head.

As we enter here, I ask You, help us come out. Give us the strength to get to the place where this person rests. And open our eyes to help us find him.

To the east, one mountain peak rose above the rest. It looked like a seated man. Montezuma Head, its called. But the Armadillos knew it by a different name.

Thats El Buddha, Mateo said, pointing across the desert. Supposedly, they left him at the foot of it.

When her brother hadnt resurfaced that Sunday in June, Lucero Salinas, a criminal lawyer, began frantically calling for help. She eventually reached the smugglers associate in Mexico, who described where Salinas had been abandoned. She passed the information to the Mexican consulate in Tucson as well as another nonprofit that searches for migrants, Aguilas del Desierto, who informed U.S. Customs and Border Protection.

There were no coordinates and only a general area, said Assistant Chief Patrol Agent Lizette Solares, head of the Tucson Sectors foreign operations branch, which includes CBPs missing migrant program.

By the time CBP got a more detailed location, a day had passed. Border Patrol agents who responded found signs of someone walking in the vicinity, but the hints soon vanished. By the third day, with updated coordinates, agents drove out in the desert on ATVs but found nothing.

We did extensive, extensive search, Solares said. It was negative.

After the CBP attempts, Ely Ortiz, co-founder of Aguilas del Desierto, decided not to search further. And he warned the Armadillos about the risks. The extreme temperatures, the remote desert it was too dangerous.

It is very, very hot where they have to walk, he said.

They came anyway.

Five days after Salinas had been abandoned, several of the Armadillos set out on foot toward the distant Buddha. The first four miles is relatively flat terrain, but in 105-degree heat, the hiking was arduous. There is no trail. They clambered across dry arroyos and through thick brush, carefully avoiding the shoe-piercing thorn balls that fall from the cholla cactus and litter the desert floor.

One of the volunteers that day, Gene OMeara, a 67-year-old retired small-business owner from Ajo, got lost in the brush. He thought hed fallen behind the group; in fact, he was in front. He spoke little Spanish. The radio chatter was confusing. He tried to scale a rise for a better vantage.

Next thing I knew I was puking, dry heaving, and just sick as hell, OMeara recalled. He lay down under a shade tree to get his heart rate to subside.

The others reached him while he was resting. Once they resumed, Lauro Lazaro, 24, heard a rattlesnake. Terrified, he quickened his pace. His heart was racing, and his head started to pound. He could find no relief.

I began to get sick from the heat, Lazaro recalled. You step and the ground is hot. Whatever rock you sit on, its hot.

OMeara helped Lazaro under a tree and gave him cold water and a wet cloth for his head. He checked his heart rate with a pulse oximeter he carries on these outings.

It wasnt long before Macias, the emergency room nurse, was gasping, his hands on his knees. He had seen heat illness cases and felt the symptoms coming on. The unsteady gait. The confusion and shortness of breath. A pounding heart.

A recipe for death

Salinas spent his first half-hour in the United States running through the desert during the hottest part of the day. To put as much distance between themselves and the border wall near Lukeville, Ariz., the migrants ran as far as they could, according to a member of the group who was a construction worker and has since been deported back to Mexico. He spoke about the journey on the condition of anonymity to detail his role in the unlawful crossing.

The group crossed just three days before President Joe Biden announced sweeping restrictions on asylum, which has led to steep reductions in illegal crossings this year.

Salinas and the four other migrants with him a woman and three men did not intend to apply for asylum and turn themselves in to the first Border Patrol officers they encountered. They were sneaking across illegally, a much more perilous path through the desert that humanitarian groups warn could become more common now that asylum has been curtailed.

Risk taking leads to loss of life. And high temperatures significantly increase that probability, said Brad Jones, a political science professor at the University of California at Davis who also works with Humane Borders, a Tucson-based nonprofit. Its really just a recipe for death.

Salinas and his group turned their cellphones off to avoid detection. They wore camouflage sun hats and backpacks to blend into the desert. Some wore boots, but Salinas showed up in black tennis shoes.

After the first grueling run, Salinas gulped down nearly an entire bottle of cold water, almost four liters, his fellow migrant recalled. Then he vomited.

The truth is it was very difficult, the construction worker recalled. One is not accustomed to this. We all felt it.

That night, they rubbed garlic on their feet and other body parts to ward off scorpions before they lay on the ground to sleep.

The next day, the group set off at first light. NASA sensors passing over this portion of the Sonoran Desert just after noon recorded temperatures up to 130 degrees radiating off the desert floor. By then, Salinas had consumed most of his water. What remained in his bottles was hot to the touch.

Around 3 p.m., the construction worker recalled, Salinas complained of a headache. Exhausted and hobbled by blistered feet, he could not keep up. He asked to rest. On the ground, in the meager shade, he passed out. The others roused him, exhorted him to continue, to give everything he had.

Salinas kept going. Late in the afternoon, they reached a plateau. The six of them had spread apart, a human chain in the desert, Salinas last of all. Then he staggered and fainted. The construction worker picked him up and hauled him the last few hundred yards to where the group would camp that night.

I carried him like he was a sack of potatoes, the construction worker recalled.

This time, Salinas was slow to revive. He slept until the early hours of the morning, finally waking in the predawn chill. That night, the construction worker cried. He was a single father with an 8-year-old son. He had risked this desert for the dream of giving his son a more dignified life. Now, he wasnt sure he would see him again.

We were all afraid, he said. All of us.

When they set off before dawn on the third day, Salinas was worse than before. He could only hobble a few minutes before stopping to rest. Somewhere beneath the Buddha, the smuggler told him, You cant go on. They were almost out of water. He was holding them back. They all might die.

The smuggler turned on his cellphone to check the map. He showed Salinas the route to the nearest road a two-hour walk, he told him, to a place where Border Patrol would pass by. The smuggler filled an empty Gatorade bottle with water and gave it to Salinas, who sat in the sand, the sun just coming up.

We left him there, the construction worker said. And we continued our walk.

Increasing danger

Roque Mateo stared down at the sun-bleached bones.

They look suspicious, he told his colleagues in early August, during his second search for Salinas.

The Armadillos regularly find old human bones, skeletons picked apart and scattered by animals. In this vast desert, many deaths go uncounted; when they are, it is often unknown how or when a migrant died. In the records collected by Humane Borders and Pima County, exposure accounted for nearly 1,500 of the 4,200 known migrant deaths in Arizona since 2000. In almost all of these cases, its going to be due to hyperthermia, Jones said and the associated lack of water.

I fear its an issue thats just going to get worse, he said.

Theres little research on whether warming temperatures have made the desert crossing more dangerous, as many who work with migrants here believe. A 2021 study in Science found that migrant deaths are disproportionately clustered in places predicted to have the highest risk of dehydration and physiological stress. And it estimated that over the next 30 years, as temperatures increase, a person would need up to a third more water to survive such a journey across the Arizona desert.

Mateo took photos of the 10-inch bone fragment, and another volunteer hung yellow police tape on a nearby bush. (A Pima County official would later determine these bones came from an animal.)

A few minutes later, his radio crackled. The volunteers had fanned out, each one about 50 yards from the next, far enough it was difficult to see one another among the cactus and brush.

A pair of vultures, someone reported.

Vultures were a good sign and could help locate remains. Migrants in distress often seek shade under trees and bushes, making them difficult to find, even at close range.

Its hard for anyone to call themselves a good searcher, Mateo said. It doesnt exist. Its luck. We are standing here, and the remains could be under that tree he indicated one close by but its impossible to check everything.

We are desperate

The Armadillos last search for Salinas happened on a day the nearby town of Ajo reached 109 degrees the hottest Sept. 7 in a record that dates back more than a century, according to the National Weather Service.

A few days before, Salinass sister, Lucero, got a call from CBP about a body. A mans remains had been found 13 miles from where her brother went missing. Decomposition meant any tattoos would not be visible. But he had black tennis shoes, the same as her brother. And their hairlines seemed to match.

On a video call, Lucero was relieved to see Nikes not her brothers brand. She looked at the mans uneven teeth, remembering her younger brothers childhood bicycle accident. The family had been pleased with the dental work and the return of her brothers uniform smile.

Lucero still believes her brother may be alive, somehow swept into the U.S. immigration detention system, under a fake or misspelled name. She has grilled the smuggler and the other migrants with him for shreds of information. She has visited fortune tellers, had her cards read, done anything to keep up hope.

We are desperate, she said.

Lucero had pleaded with her brother not to go not to give up his family and his country for a place and a language he did not know. He chose to follow the same route taken by their father, who had lived in the United States on and off for years.

I told him, Please, brother, imagine if something happens to you? she recalled. Who is going to look for you?

The Armadillos walked nearly 20 miles on their last search. They would find old backpacks, discarded clothes, empty water jugs the familiar detritus of migration. But no sign of Salinas.

At the hottest times, as the heat emanated off the ground and enveloped them, a disorienting and malignant force, they would drop into the sand, under any scrap of shade, heaving from the exertion. Then they would get up and walk on.

This summer, Salinass father has begged the smuggler to take him back, alone, into the desert. He will pay, he says. He needs to know where they left his son.

But the smuggler has so far refused. Maybe November, maybe later, he has told the family. Not until the desert cools down.
